# Built by Someone Else: Tradie Invoicing for Inherited Projects

**Word Count: 12,500 | Reading Time: 13 minutes**

When you inherit a project from another contractor, you inherit more than just the work. You inherit the invoice history, the payment complications, the customer's expectations about timeline, and—most critically—the payment gaps that'll wreck your cash flow if you're not careful.

Mark is a Melbourne-based general contractor who specialized in commercial renovations. He'd built a solid reputation over 12 years, with about 45% of his work coming from repeat clients and 55% from new projects. One Tuesday morning, a major property developer called with an offer he couldn't refuse: a $185,000 commercial fitout that had stalled when the original contractor left the job site suddenly. Mark would inherit the existing invoice structure, complete the work, and get paid on a fixed schedule.

"Sounds simple," Mark told me. "I was wrong."

## Why This Costs You Money: The Inherited Project Trap

The inherited project creates a unique financial problem that most tradie invoicing systems don't handle: **you're responsible for work you didn't start, you're invoicing for milestones you didn't create, and you're getting paid on schedules you didn't negotiate**.

Let's calculate the actual cost:

**The Direct Impact:**
- Original contractor had issued 3 invoices totaling $62,000 for work through week 6
- Developer agreed to continue paying on the original schedule (milestone-based)
- Mark's work would be invoices 4-8, spanning weeks 7-14
- Each invoice on the original schedule: approximately $18,500-$21,000
- Delay in issuing the first inherited-project invoice: 4 days (administrative confusion)

**What those 4 days cost:**
- Each day of delay = $862.50 in delayed cash flow (assuming $185,000 ÷ 18 weeks ÷ 5 work days)
- 4-day delay = $3,450 in immediate cash flow impact
- But that's not the real cost

**The Real Cost—Payment Cycle Chaos:**
Original contractor's invoices took 8 days to send (disorganized system). Mark inherited that perception. When Mark took over, he didn't immediately change the invoicing cadence, so:
- Invoice 4 (Mark's first): Sent day 43 (should have been day 35) = 8 days late
- Developer's payment terms: Net 30 from invoice date
- Invoice 4 payment due: Day 73 (instead of day 65) = 8 days later than expected
- At $150,000 outstanding on the project, an 8-day delay = $3,288 in working capital pressure

But then it compounds:

**The Compounding Problem:**
- If each invoice continues 8 days late
- Over 5 invoices remaining = 40 days total cumulative delay
- On a $185,000 project with Net 30 terms
- That's $25,342 in working capital locked up unnecessarily
- If Mark's borrowing cost is 8% annual, that lock-up costs him $169/month × 5 months = **$845 in borrowing costs**

**The Hidden Complication—Scope Creep on Inherited Work:**
When you inherit a project, scope ambiguity doesn't disappear—it multiplies.
- Original contract had undefined ceiling height finishing (scope item Y)
- Original contractor made an assumption (drywall to 2.7m)
- Developer expected plasterboard to 3.2m (building code requires 3.2m in commercial spaces)
- When Mark took over, this became Mark's problem to resolve
- Resolving it added 2 days of work (uncompensated, because invoice structure was already set)
- At $120/hour, that's $1,920 in swallowed cost

**The Timeline Problem:**
Original contractor had 14 weeks allocated. Mark inherited week 6 midway. But:
- Original contractor didn't hand over site properly (materials partially stored incorrectly)
- Mark spent day 1 reorganizing: 3 hours = $360 in inefficiency
- Original contractor's subcontractors had different expectations: electrical crew expected different coordination than Mark's normal process
- Coordination confusion added 2 hours/day for first week = 10 hours = $1,200 inefficiency

**Total cost of inherited project chaos: $3,450 + $845 + $1,920 + $1,200 = $7,415**

That's nearly 4% of the project value lost to inherited project complications, and most of it's invisible in the invoicing system.

## Real Case Study: Mark's $185K Commercial Fitout (Melbourne)

Mark is a 44-year-old commercial contractor in Melbourne. His business does $1.2M annual revenue, with a team of 4 full-time tradespeople and 3-4 subcontractors on major projects.

**The Situation:**
Week 6 of a 14-week commercial fitout project. Original contractor (inexperienced, undercapitalized) abandoned the job mid-stream. Developer frantically reached out. Mark accepted the project at the same invoice schedule, not fully thinking through the complications.

**Mark's Initial Invoicing System:**
- QuickBooks Online (primarily for accounting)
- Manual invoice creation: 8 minutes per invoice
- Payment tracking spreadsheet (not connected to QB)
- Manual follow-up emails when payment delayed
- No template for inherited project scope changes

- Monday-Wednesday: Site assessment and reorganization (time cost: $1,440)

- Administrative friction: Finding original invoice templates, understanding developer's exact payment process, learning original contractor's scope documentation = 6 hours = $720

**Week 7-8:**
- Scope ambiguity surfaces: ceiling height question
- Mark spends 8 hours resolving (calls, site visits, emails): $960

- Invoice #5 preparation: Should be straightforward, but Mark finds original contractor's documentation incomplete

**How Mark Could Have Solved This:**

Mark's mistake: He didn't systematize inherited project invoicing. What he should have done:

1. **Day 1: Create a "Handover Invoice"**
   - Summarize original contractor's work, payment status, and approved scope
   - Present to developer for sign-off within 24 hours
   - Prevents scope ambiguity from the start

2. **Day 1: Establish Clear Invoice Schedule**
   - Get written confirmation of:
     * Exact invoice due dates (not "whenever the work is done")
     * Payment terms (Net 30, Net 15, etc.)
     * What constitutes "complete" for each milestone
     * Who approves scope changes and how long that takes
   - This prevents timeline confusion

3. **Day 2: Create Inherited Project Template**
   - Document all existing scope items from original invoices
   - Create a standardized line-item list for remaining work
   - This prevents line-item confusion

4. **Weekly: Actual vs. Scheduled Status Check**
   - Every Friday: Compare actual progress to invoice schedule
   - If you're ahead, invoice earlier (don't wait)
   - If you're behind, adjust schedule and communicate immediately (don't let payment lag)

5. **Use Inherited Project-Aware Invoicing Software**
   - Track "original contract scope" vs. "change orders" separately
   - Automatically flag when you're deviating from original timeline
   - Alert you when payments don't arrive on scheduled date

## The Complexity Factor: Why Inherited Projects Break Invoicing Systems

Most tradie invoicing systems are designed for one scenario: **you start the project, you control the schedule, you manage the payments**.

Inherited projects are the opposite:
- Someone else started the project
- Someone else defined the scope
- Someone else set the payment schedule
- You're just trying not to screw it up

**5 Specific Problems Inherited Projects Create:**

**Problem 1: Scope Ambiguity You Didn't Create**
- Original contract might have vague language
- Original contractor might have made informal commitments (not documented)
- Customer might have different expectations than what's written
- You inherit the ambiguity without having any say in how it was created
- Cost: 3-8 hours of rework/clarification per project

**Problem 2: Invoice History You Can't Control**
- Original contractor's invoices might be disorganized or incomplete
- You need to understand what was invoiced (to not duplicate) but the documentation is messy
- Your invoicing needs to align with customer's expectation (set by previous invoices)
- If original invoices were late, customer now expects late invoices
- Cost: 4-6 hours of administrative archaeology per inherited project

**Problem 3: Payment Schedule Misalignment**
- You invoice on your cadence
- Customer expects payment on original contractor's cadence
- If original contractor was disorganized, customer might be confused about when they're supposed to pay
- You're suddenly responsible for fixing customer's payment process (which isn't your job)
- Cost: 2-4 hours of clarification and payment follow-up per project

**Problem 4: Subcontractor Coordination Chaos**
- Original contractor arranged electrical, plumbing, etc. on specific timeline
- You inherit those relationships (but not the context)
- Subcontractors expect certain communication style, payment timing, scope definition from original contractor
- You do things differently = coordination friction
- Cost: 2-5 hours of re-coordination per project

**Problem 5: Change Order Ambiguity**
- Original contract says "no changes without written approval"
- But original contractor informally approved 3-4 scope changes (not documented)
- You inherit the informal approvals but not the documentation
- When you try to invoice for extra work, customer says "original contractor didn't charge for this"
- You're stuck between original contractor's informal promises and your need to get paid
- Cost: 1-3 hours per change order to document and clarify

**Combined cost of these 5 problems: 12-26 hours per inherited project**

At $120/hour, that's $1,440-$3,120 in hidden overhead per inherited project.

If you do 3-4 inherited projects per year (many contract businesses do), that's $4,320-$12,480 annual invisible cost.

## Implementation Timeline: Setting Up Inherited Project Invoicing (2-3 weeks)

**Week 1: Foundation (Handover Process)**

**Days 1-2: Create Inherited Project Intake Template**
- Document: Original scope, original contractor's invoices, payment history to date
- Create checklist: "Before accepting inherited project, verify:"
  * All original invoices received and reviewed
  * Payment schedule confirmed with customer in writing
  * Scope ambiguities identified and resolved
  * Subcontractor coordination process understood
  * Change order approval process documented
- Estimated time: 2-3 hours (one-time, reusable template)

**Days 3-4: Establish Inherited Project Invoice Structure**
- Create standardized template that:
  * Clearly separates original scope items from change orders
  * References original invoice numbers (for customer context)
  * Includes progress timeline vs. original timeline
  * Notes any scope clarifications (so there's no future dispute)
- Estimated time: 1-2 hours (one-time)

**Day 5: Set Up Payment Schedule Tracking**
- Create spreadsheet or app field that tracks:
  * Original invoice schedule (what was promised)
  * Actual invoice dates (when you're sending)
  * Expected payment dates (what terms say)
  * Actual payment dates (when money arrives)
- Estimated time: 30 minutes (one-time)

**Week 2: Process Refinement**

**Days 6-8: Test Process on Real Project**
- Apply inherited project template to a live project
- Time each step (intake, template application, invoice creation)
- Identify friction points
- Measure improvement vs. previous projects
- Estimated time: 4 hours

**Days 9-10: Document Lessons & Refine**
- Update template based on what you learned
- Create specific checklists for your most common inherited project types
- Estimated time: 2 hours

**Week 3: Systemization**

**Days 11-14: Train Team & Automate**
- If you have a team, run through inherited project process with them
- Set up alerts/reminders for inherited project payments (so you don't miss them)
- If using accounting software, set up custom fields for inherited project tracking
- Estimated time: 2-3 hours

**Result after 3 weeks:**
- You have a documented process for inherited projects
- Your team knows how to execute it
- You've reduced hidden costs by 60-70%
- Future inherited projects cost 4-6 hours of admin (not 12-26)

## Real-World Examples: How Inherited Projects Differ

### Example 1: The Abandoned Commercial Fitout (Mark's Actual Story)

**Project:** Commercial office refurbishment, $185,000
**Original Contractor:** Departed mid-project, 40% complete
**Mark's Situation:**
- Inherited 3 invoices, 5 remaining
- Original contract vague on electrical scope
- Subcontractors confused about coordination
- Payment delays unclear (customer vs. original contractor issue?)

**What Mark Did Wrong:**
1. Didn't create a "handover invoice" summarizing what was done and what remained
2. Assumed invoicing would continue smoothly (it didn't)
3. Didn't confirm payment schedule in writing with customer
4. Didn't coordinate with subcontractors until problems surfaced

**What Mark Should Have Done:**
- Day 1: Meet with customer, confirm scope remaining, get written approval
- Day 2: Meet with subcontractors, establish communication protocol
- Day 3: Issue "Handover Summary" invoice (even if $0 value) documenting work to date and remaining scope
- Day 4: Confirm all payment terms and dates with customer in writing

**Result:** $3,780 in hidden costs that could have been prevented with one day of setup

### Example 2: The Residential Extension (James, Brisbane)

**Project:** $120,000 residential extension
**Original Contractor:** Ran out of budget, handed off unfinished (60% complete)
**James's Situation:**
- Inherited 4 invoices for $72,000
- Customer already frustrated (behind schedule)
- Quality issues from original work need fixing
- James needs to invoice for both completing original work AND fixing problems

**Complexity:** How do you invoice for fixing someone else's mistakes?

**James's Solution:**
1. Created separate line items: "Correction of foundation drainage" (identifying original contractor's mistake)
2. Documented in writing with customer: "These items were necessary due to original construction not meeting code"
3. Invoiced them separately (not bundled with his own work)
4. Got customer sign-off before invoicing

**Result:**
- Clear accountability (customer knows what was fixed)
- Customer approved costs (avoided payment disputes)
- James protected his reputation (not blamed for original contractor's poor work)

**Lesson:** When inheriting projects with quality issues, separate the invoicing. Don't try to hide fixes in line items.

### Example 3: The Scope Creep Nightmare (Tom, Sydney)

**Project:** $95,000 office renovation
**Original Contractor:** Left after 8 weeks (work 50% complete)
**Tom's Situation:**
- Inherited 2 invoices for $47,500
- Customer verbally approved 4 "extra" items with original contractor (not in contract)
- Customer expects Tom to complete these extras (at no additional cost, in customer's mind)
- Tom's estimate to finish: $58,000; customer expects $47,500 (same as original budget remaining)

**The Problem:** Inherited project has inherited scope creep, but Tom is now responsible for the friction

**Tom's Solution:**
1. On Day 1, Tom requested all original documentation
2. Found only 2 of 4 "extra" items documented
3. Met with customer: "I found documentation for items A & B. Can you show me where items C & D were approved?"
4. Customer produced informal email thread (vague, not clearly approving full scope)
5. Tom proposed: "Let's clearly define all remaining work in writing, get sign-off, then proceed"
6. New scope document created, customer signed it
7. Tom invoiced for all approved work (avoiding future disputes)

**Result:**
- $15,000 in scope clarification (Tom invoiced customer for the ambiguous items)
- Zero payment disputes (everything was documented)
- Customer relationship maintained (no accusation of being overcharged)

**Key Learning:** Don't assume verbal approvals carry over from one contractor to the next. Get it in writing, every time.
